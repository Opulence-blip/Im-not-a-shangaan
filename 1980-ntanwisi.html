Black Homeland Leaders Speak Out Against Police Violence (1976)

In 1976, during a period of severe unrest in South Africa, leaders of homelands—territories set up under apartheid to segregate black communities—met in Johannesburg.They issued statements, criticizing police brutality against protesting black students.

  • Context of the Riots: The unrest followed the Soweto student protests, where young people resisted the forced use of Afrikaans in schools. Over 250 people lost their lives during these demonstrations.
  • Leaders’ Response: They expressed support for peaceful protests, condemned the destruction of property, and strongly criticized the violent actions of the police.
  • Message to the Government: The leaders pointed out that denying fundamental human rights to black South Africans had led to unrest, implying that the only “language” the government seemed to understand was force.


The 1976 statement by the homeland leaders marked a turning point, showing that they were no longer merely passive supporters of the apartheid regime. It emphasized the importance and influence of youth-led protests as a driving force against oppression, while also drawing both national and international attention to the severe realities of apartheid policing and governance.

Homeland Leaders Reject Separate Advisory Council (1980)

In 1980, discussions about a new constitutional arrangement for South Africa’s black majority encountered a significant obstacle. Leaders from the homelands, semi-autonomous territories created under apartheid, rejected the idea of a separate advisory

  • Background: The President’s Council was designed as an advisory body composed of white, coloured, and Indian representatives only, deliberately excluding black participation through a separate council.
  • Response from Homeland Leaders: By rejecting this proposal, the homeland leaders resisted an attempt to sideline black voices in constitutional matters.
  • Key Figure: Professor Hudson Ntsanwisi of Gazankulu was one of the homeland leaders who spoke during these discussions, emphasizing the need for meaningful participation rather than token representation.
