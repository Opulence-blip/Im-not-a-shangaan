Samora Machel

Documentary and interview with the former Mozambican military commander and political leader. A socialist in the tradition of Marxism–Leninism, he served as the first President of Mozambique from the country’s independence in 1975.



The Day of the Crash - October 19, 1986

On the night of October 19, 1986, Samora Machel, President of Mozambique, was returning home from an international meeting in Zambia aboard a Soviet-built Tupolev Tu-134 aircraft. He was accompanied by a delegation of Mozambican officials and Soviet crew members. As the plane neared Mbuzini, a village in the Lebombo Mountains in eastern South Africa, it crashed into a hillside, killing 35 of the 44 people on board, including Machel.

The crash happened just a few kilometers inside South African territory, near the borders of Mozambique, South Africa, and Eswatini (Swaziland). The weather was clear, and the crash occurred in the late evening. The aircraft was off course — instead of following the normal flight path to Maputo, the capital of Mozambique, it diverted and descended into mountainous terrain.



The Monument of Samora Machel

The Samora Machel Monument, located in Mbuzini, South Africa, commemorates the life and tragic death of Samora Machel.

he monument, unveiled by Nelson Mandela in 1999, features 35 steel tubes that produce a haunting sound in the wind, symbolizing the voices of those who perished. It stands as a powerful tribute to Machel’s legacy as a liberation hero and a symbol of unity between South Africa and Mozambique.

The crash has long been suspected to involve foul play by the apartheid regime, though its exact cause remains uncertain.
