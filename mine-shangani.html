First sculpture

Shangaan

The concept of Shangaan was first developed in the artist’s studio in Doornfontein, Johannesburg, in 1907. After relocating from Pretoria and securing support from investors to pursue his own projects, Van Wouw experienced a particularly productive and inventive period during that year.



The sculpture of the young Shangaan man conveys a strong sense of defiance. With his head leaning forward, brow furrowed, eyes narrowed, lips pressed together, and arms crossed with fingers tucked under his armpits to emphasize his biceps, the figure is both striking and skillfully crafted. Beyond its artistic impact, the piece reflects the challenging lives of newly urbanized laborers working in the Witwatersrand mines, where over 60% of underground miners were Shangaan migrants, mostly from southern Mozambique. Another version of the Shangaan sculpture, cast at Giovanni Nisini’s foundry in Rome, eventually became part of Sir Arthur Conan Doyle’s collection, the famous author and Boer War medic.



Second sculpture

The Mealipap Eater



"The Mealiepap Eater" portrays a Shangaan man enjoying mealiepap from a traditional three-legged pot. The same individual also served as the model for Van Wouw’s sculpture of a tribesman titled “Shangaan.” Mealiepap, a staple food made from ground maize, can be prepared as a soft porridge or a firmer pap, typically eaten alongside meat or vegetables.
